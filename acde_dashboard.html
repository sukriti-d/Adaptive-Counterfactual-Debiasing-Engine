Counterfactual Instability Analysis

Measures how predictions change when protected attributes are flipped — a fair model shows near-zero instability

⬡
What is counterfactual instability?
For each individual, we create a "counterfactual twin" — identical in skills, experience and qualifications, but with a different protected attribute (e.g., Male→Female). If the model's prediction changes, it reveals direct discriminatory sensitivity. The Flip Rate measures how often this happens. The IDI Score measures the average probability shift magnitude. A perfectly fair model would show 0% instability.

Bias Explanation Panel

Plain-language interpretation of fairness audit results — designed for non-technical stakeholders and decision-makers

🚨
CRITICAL BIAS LEVEL DETECTED (PRE-MITIGATION)
Composite Fairness Score: 25.9% / 100% — severely below acceptable threshold
DPD: 96.3pp Flip: 67.2% EOV: 99.8pp
⚠
Intersectional Bias Detected
The model shows a 96.3 percentage-point gap in positive outcome rates between the most and least favored groups. Male/Race-A applicants receive positive outcomes 96.3% of the time, while Female/Race-C applicants receive them 0% of the time — despite potentially equal qualifications. This extreme disparity is consistent with severe algorithmic discrimination compounded by intersectionality.
⬡
Counterfactual Discrimination Found
When we create "counterfactual twins" — identical individuals except for their gender — 67.2% of predictions change. This means 1,344 out of 2,000 individuals would receive a different outcome purely because of their gender, not their qualifications. The IDI Score of 0.522 confirms the model's confidence shifts dramatically based solely on protected attributes.
⚖
Equalized Odds Violation
The model's True Positive Rate differs by 99.8 percentage points across groups (Male/A TPR: 99.8% vs Female/C TPR: 0%). This means equally qualified individuals from different demographic groups have vastly different chances of being correctly identified. The disparate impact ratio (0.018) is far below the legal 4/5ths threshold (0.80) — indicating legally significant disparate impact under most fairness regulations.
✦
Mitigation Results
After IPW + iterative reweighting, the Composite Fairness Score improved from 25.9% to 68.1% (+42.2pts). DPD dropped by 46.1%, EOV reduced by 60.6%, and the flip rate fell from 67.2% to 32.2%. The accuracy cost was minimal at 2.6 percentage points, demonstrating that fairness and predictive performance are not mutually exclusive — bias reduction is achievable without gutting model utility.
🎓 What This Means in Real-World Terms
Imagine 2,000 job applications being scored by this AI hiring system. Under the original model, 1,344 applicants would receive a different hiring decision if only their gender were changed — not their skills, experience, or education. That's direct algorithmic discrimination affecting two-thirds of all applicants. After mitigation, this drops to approximately 644 applicants — a 52% reduction in discriminatory decisions. However, complete fairness was not achieved (score: 68.1%), and this system should not be deployed without additional oversight, human review, and continued monitoring.
Actionable Recommendations
Critical
Deploy only the debiased model
The baseline model (fairness score 25.9%) shows unacceptable bias. Only the mitigated model (68.1%) should be used for real decisions affecting individuals.
High
Monitor ongoing fairness in production
Even after mitigation, disparate impact ratio (0.1427) remains far below the 0.80 threshold. Continuous monitoring with periodic bias audits is required.
High
Investigate data collection bias
Historical bias in training data is a root cause. Audit data collection and labeling practices to prevent perpetuating systemic inequities in future model versions.
Medium
Explore in-processing debiasing techniques
IPW is a post-hoc technique. Adversarial debiasing or fairness constraints during training (e.g., Zafar et al. 2017) may achieve better joint fairness–utility tradeoffs.
Low
Document and disclose bias audit findings
Transparency is both ethically important and increasingly required by regulation (EU AI Act, US EEOC guidelines, NYC Local Law 144). Publish audit reports annually.
